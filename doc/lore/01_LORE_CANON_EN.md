# The Great Imbalance

## The Dying Core

For millennia, the galaxy breathed in a cycle of expansion and decay. Civilizations rose around young stars, consumed their abundance, and vanished into the dark between systems.

Then the core stars began to cool.

The decline was slow enough to be measured across generations, yet fast enough to make every long-term future impossible. Habitable zones contracted. Ancient energy networks failed. Entire sectors discovered that survival was no longer a question of territory, but of thermodynamics.

From the dying embers of the old empires emerged two radical answers.

Neither side was born from simple malice. Both were shaped by fear, calculation, and a sincere conviction that only one definition of life could survive the end of the galaxy.

---

## The Cryos Union

### Doctrine: The Eternal Stillness

The Cryos Union believes that heat is the oldest form of disorder.

To Cryos philosophers, every flame is a clock, every moving machine an act of waste, and every living metabolism a debt paid to entropy. Their answer is not extinction, but preservation through control.

Cryos terraformers lower planetary temperatures toward engineered near-zero ecosystems. Oceans become crystalline reservoirs. Atmospheres are thinned and stabilized. Cities descend beneath reflective ice, where computation, memory, and biological life continue at carefully rationed speed.

The Union does not describe this process as conquest.

It calls it mercy.

A frozen world can endure. A burning world can only spend itself.

Cryos commanders therefore see the war as a rescue operation conducted on a galactic scale. Every seized planet is removed from the chaos of heat and placed into a state designed to survive indefinitely.

Their enemies call them tomb-builders.

Cryos answers that a tomb is still preferable to oblivion.

---

## The Ignis Hegemony

### Doctrine: The Radiant Pulse

The Ignis Hegemony rejects preservation without motion.

Its doctrine begins with a simple claim: life is not a shape, a memory, or a stored pattern. Life is activity. It is friction, circulation, hunger, reaction, and change.

Where Cryos sees heat as death, Ignis sees energy as the only possible escape.

Ignis planetary engines crack open crusts, redirect mantle pressure, and turn conquered worlds into lava-rich industrial furnaces. These worlds feed orbital shipyards, stellar ignition arrays, and the immense propulsion projects intended to carry civilization beyond the cooling galactic core.

The Hegemony does not promise permanence.

It promises momentum.

To Ignis strategists, the Cryos Union is building an eternal civilization that will never truly live. The Hegemony instead seeks enough energy to outrun collapse, even if every step consumes another world.

Cryos calls them arsonists.

Ignis answers that fire is the first proof that darkness has not yet won.

---

## The Grey Frontier

Between the blue advance and the red expansion lie the Grey Worlds.

They are neutral forest planets, the last broad reservoirs of unmodified biological diversity. Their seas still circulate naturally. Their climates still shift without planetary regulators. Their ecosystems contain species that survived outside either faction's engineered doctrine.

In another age, such worlds would have been sanctuaries.

In the age of the Great Imbalance, they are strategic resources.

Both factions consider their natural state wasteful.

Cryos colonization freezes forests into preserved carbon vaults and converts living continents into stable cryogenic ecosystems.

Ignis colonization strips the same worlds for biomass, minerals, and geothermal ignition, reshaping them into volcanic powerhouses.

The Grey Frontier is therefore not neutral because it is protected.

It is neutral only because neither side has reached it yet.

---

## The Orbital Shipyards

Every controlled planet supports an orbital shipyard.

These shipyards descend from a standardized military architecture created by the final interstellar empires before the Great Imbalance. That shared inheritance explains why both factions deploy the same broad hull classes.

A Cryos Fighter and an Ignis Fighter may differ in cooling systems, reactor philosophy, materials, and visual design, but they occupy the same tactical role.

The war is not fought with completely alien technologies.

It is fought with mirrored tools serving opposing ideas.

---

## The War of Temperature

Territory has become a measurement of temperature.

A blue planet is stabilized, frozen, and preserved.

A red planet is ignited, industrialized, and accelerated.

A grey planet still belongs to itself, but only temporarily.

Victory is no longer measured by lives saved or civilizations liberated. It is measured by which thermal doctrine controls enough worlds to define the future.

> “In this war, victory isn't measured in lives saved, but in degrees of temperature. We aren't fighting for morality; we are fighting for the very definition of existence.”
>
> — Lost Signal, Sector Zero

## Canonical tone

The setting should avoid a simple good-versus-evil structure.

Both factions:

- believe they are preserving civilization;
- destroy natural worlds in the process;
- consider compromise physically impossible;
- use morally coherent but extreme reasoning;
- are willing to sacrifice the present for a different form of future.
